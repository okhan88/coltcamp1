Ali Thanwi (1862–1943) often referred to honorifically as Ḥakīm al-Ummat, was a central figure of Islamic religious, spiritual, and intellectual life in late-nineteenth- and twentieth-century South Asia and continues to be highly influential today.[2] He was an author, jurist and a Sufi mentor of the Chishti order. He was an alumnus of the Darul Uloom Deoband, and authored several hundred books including Bayan Ul Quran and Bahishti Zewar. His reworking of traditional Islamic knowledge and spiritualization of Shariah to make it accessible to the common people had a direct impact on modern Islam. Thus Tablighi Jamaat, the chief transnational Islamic movement with between 100 and 150 million followers that leads the spiritual education of ordinary Muslims, is considerably inspired by His writings and activities.[3]
